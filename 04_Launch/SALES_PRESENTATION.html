The challenges

What the regulatory template asks for—and where it gets heavy

Regulators expect one risk assessment per preparation (including different strengths), a 12‑month review of each record with supervisor sign-off, and assessment of cumulative risk across all compounds. The thinking is important. The duplication is heavy.

📋

120 formulations = 120 assessments

Same ingredient, same hazard profile, same room—but change the strength and you start a new document. Scale that across 50+ preparations and you’re maintaining dozens of assessments that are largely identical in structure and reasoning.

🔄

Same content, rewritten again and again

Ventilation standards, PPE logic, environmental controls—rewritten for every preparation. The template is thorough; in practice you’re copying the same rationales across forms. That’s not theoretical work. That’s hours.

📅

12‑month reviews become paperwork logistics

Every assessment reviewed annually, signed and dated. On paper that means tracking cycles manually, updating SDS references, printing revisions, archiving old copies, and chasing signatures. Governance starts to feel like box-checking.

⚠️

Volume changes behaviour

When supervisors juggle cumulative exposure, MFR updates, and quality control, the question can shift from “Is my reasoning solid?” to “Is every box filled?” That’s human—and that’s the real operational risk.

Sources of our data

We use the same references you would—automatically

Hazard and compliance data come from official, traceable sources. The system doesn’t guess; it looks up the same sources the pharmacist would use manually.

NIOSH Hazardous Drug List

Government classification of hazardous drugs (Table 1, 2, 3) for PPE, ventilation, and facility level. Auto-tagged on ingredients.

Safety Data Sheets (SDS)

Manufacturer hazard documents per ingredient: specific hazards, ventilation, gloves, handling. Required for defensible rationales.

Health Canada DPD / Product Monograph

Official drug identification, warnings, and contraindications. Links to the official monograph for rationale documentation.

WHMIS / GHS

Standardized Canadian workplace hazard classifications: pictograms, signal words, hazard statements (e.g. H350: May cause cancer).

NAPRA Model Standards

National compounding standards: preparation complexity (simple, moderate, complex), facility level, and required controls.

Regulatory templates (OCP) & USP <795>

Format and required fields for risk assessments; Beyond-Use Dating, quality control, and complexity definitions.
